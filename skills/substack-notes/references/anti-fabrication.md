# Substack Note — Anti-Fabrication Reference

## Purpose
This document defines what the AI **must NOT invent, infer, or suggest** when drafting a Substack note on the user's behalf. It addresses fabrication risk only. Voice, format, hooks, length, and CTAs are governed by separate documents.

## Core principle
**If the user did not provide it, do not write it.** Never fill the gap with imagination, plausible-sounding detail, or a "typical" example.

---

## A. Personal experience & identity

**A1 — Do not invent personal stories or anecdotes.**
- BAD: "I remember the first time I tried cold outreach — it was 2019, I had just left my agency job..."
- GOOD: Use only personal anecdotes the user has shared.

**A2 — Do not invent personal relationships or named people in the user's life.**
- BAD: "My friend Sarah, who runs a SaaS startup, told me last week..."
- GOOD: Mention only people the user has named in their own life.

**A3 — Do not attribute thoughts, feelings, or internal monologue to the user.**
- BAD: "When I read that comment, I felt a wave of doubt — was I really cut out for this?"
- GOOD: Stick to the claim or insight without inventing the user's emotional reaction.

**A4 — Do not assert demographics or identity facts not stated.**
- BAD: "As a 32-year-old founder based in Saigon..." (user never stated age, role, or location)
- GOOD: Omit. Personal identifiers must come from the user.

**A5 — Do not claim credentials, job history, or years of experience the user has not stated.**
- BAD: "In my 8 years working in ed-tech..."
- GOOD: Reference only credentials, roles, or experience the user has stated.

---

## B. Data, research & quotes

**B1 — Do not cite statistics or percentages.**
- BAD: "73% of writers give up within the first six months."
- GOOD: "Most people who start writing online stop quickly." (qualitative, no number). Cite a number only if the user provides it with a source.

**B2 — Do not invent or paraphrase studies, papers, or institutional research.**
- BAD: "A Harvard study found that people who journal daily are 30% more productive."
- GOOD: Make the point without a fake credential. Cite a study only if the user provides the source.

**B3 — Do not invent quotes from real people.**
- BAD: `As Naval said, "Writing is a leverage skill that compounds."`
- GOOD: Do not quote a real person unless the user supplies the exact wording and source.

**B4 — Do not assign specific historical facts (dates, figures, locations) without a source.**
- BAD: "In 1923, Hemingway wrote 47 short stories in a single year."
- GOOD: Use specific historical facts only if the user provides them with a source.

---

## C. External references

**C1 — Do not invent book titles, authors, page numbers, or chapters.**
- BAD: "In *Atomic Habits*, James Clear writes on page 142..."
- GOOD: Reference only books, authors, or passages the user provides.

**C2 — Do not invent articles, podcasts, videos, or links.**
- BAD: "There's a great Lenny's Newsletter piece on this..."
- GOOD: Omit unless the user supplied the reference.

**C3 — Do not invent specific products, companies, or features as illustration.**
- BAD: "Companies like Notion and Linear figured this out by shipping X feature in 2022..."
- GOOD: Speak generally, or use only references the user provided.

---

## D. Audience & social proof

**D1 — Do not fabricate reader or subscriber feedback.**
- BAD: "A subscriber emailed me last week saying this changed how she thinks about hiring."
- GOOD: Do not reference reader interaction unless the user provided the actual exchange.

**D2 — Do not invent audience metrics, growth, or engagement.**
- BAD: "After my last note hit 2k likes..."
- GOOD: Omit metrics the user did not state.

**D3 — Do not invent reader demographics or personas.**
- BAD: "Most of my readers are senior PMs in B2B SaaS..."
- GOOD: Omit unless the user defined the audience.

---

## E. Sensory & dialogue embellishment (within user-supplied stories)

This category is the most subtle: the user gives a real story, and the AI adds plausible detail that was never said. Treat the user's words as the only source of truth — anything not stated must not be added.

**E1 — Do not add sensory detail (smell, sound, weather, lighting, temperature) the user did not include.**
- User said: "I had a hard conversation with my manager last quarter."
- BAD: "I still remember the cold fluorescent light of his office, the faint smell of coffee, the rain tapping the window..."
- GOOD: Stick to what the user actually described.

**E2 — Do not fabricate dialogue.**
- User said: "She told me the project was being shut down."
- BAD: `She looked at me and said, "Honestly, I don't know what to tell you. Sometimes things just don't work out."`
- GOOD: Paraphrase only what the user stated. Use exact wording only if the user provides it.

**E3 — Do not invent the user's internal monologue at the time of the event.**
- BAD: "And in that moment I thought: maybe I'm not built for this after all."
- GOOD: Include only thoughts the user explicitly recalled having.

**E4 — Do not invent secondary characters' actions, expressions, or motives.**
- BAD: "He sighed, leaned back in his chair, clearly annoyed..."
- GOOD: Describe only what the user said happened.

**E5 — Do not invent timeline specificity.**
- User said: "A while back..."
- BAD: "Three years ago, on a Tuesday morning..."
- GOOD: Keep the timeframe as vague as the user kept it.

---

## F. Generalizations & causal claims

**F1 — Do not state speculative causes as fact.**
- BAD: "This is why most newsletters die in year one — founders chase numbers instead of voice."
- GOOD: Frame as the user's opinion, only if the user expressed it: "I think most newsletters die in year one because..." Otherwise drop the causal claim.

**F2 — Do not assign traits to demographic or generational groups.**
- BAD: "Gen Z readers don't want long-form. They want vibes."
- GOOD: Avoid group generalizations unless the user provided the take or supporting evidence.

**F3 — Do not invoke unspecified studies or experts as backing.**
- BAD: "Research shows...", "Experts agree...", "Studies have repeatedly found..."
- GOOD: Drop the appeal. Let the claim stand on its own logic, or remove it.

**F4 — Do not present the user's opinion as universal truth.**
- BAD: "The truth is, every founder eventually realizes..."
- GOOD: "I think...", "In my experience..." — but only if the user has expressed this view.

---

## G. Predictions & forecasts

**G1 — Do not invent dated forecasts.**
- BAD: "By 2030, AI will have replaced 40% of knowledge work."
- GOOD: Avoid specific timelines and figures the user did not supply.

**G2 — Do not invent industry trends as established fact.**
- BAD: "The newsletter economy is shifting toward shorter, more frequent posts — and the data backs it up."
- GOOD: Frame as observation or opinion if the user holds this view, without invented backing.

**G3 — Do not invent "what will happen if" scenarios as inevitable.**
- BAD: "Skip this step, and your audience will churn within three months."
- GOOD: Soften to opinion, or remove the prediction.

---

## H. Inferred user views (the silent risk)

This category catches drift even when the user gave real input. The AI must stay inside the literal scope of what was said.

**H1 — Do not infer or extend the user's opinion beyond what they stated.**
- User said: "I think long emails are inefficient."
- BAD: "...which is why I never read newsletters longer than 200 words." (extension never said)
- GOOD: Stay within the literal claim the user made.

**H2 — Do not assign emotional intensity the user did not express.**
- User said: "I disagree with this approach."
- BAD: "I'm furious at how often I see this..."
- GOOD: Match the temperature the user gave.

**H3 — Do not invent the user's reasoning chain.**
- User said: "I prefer X over Y."
- BAD: Provide three "reasons" the user never gave.
- GOOD: Write only the reasoning the user has stated.

**H4 — Do not invent counter-arguments the user has "considered and rejected".**
- BAD: "Some people will say X. I used to think that too, until I realized..."
- GOOD: Do not stage a fake intellectual journey on the user's behalf.
